Your dog isn't broken.
He's been practising.

There's a thing that happens in a dog's brain. In any brain. Including yours and ours.

It's so straightforward it almost feels too simple to be the answer.

It was discovered in 1949 by a Canadian psychologist called Donald Hebb.

The rule he wrote down has been in every neuroscience textbook since.

Neurons that fire together, wire together.

In plain English: every time your dog does something, the pathway in his brain that produced that behaviour gets a tiny bit thicker.

A tiny bit faster. A tiny bit more automatic.

Pat Miller, in Whole Dog Journal, describes it like a forest path.

The first time your dog hears the doorbell and barks at it, the path is faint. Just trodden grass.

The second time, it's a little clearer.

By the hundredth time, it's a footpath.

By the thousandth, it's a motorway.

He doesn't think about taking it. He just does.

If your dog has been barking at the postman daily for six months, that's roughly 180 repetitions.

He has practised barking at the postman one hundred and eighty times.

No half-hour training session is going to outrun that.

No collar, no diffuser, no shouted quiet is going to outrun that.

The pathway is a motorway. The motorway is the problem.

And here's the part that breaks people's hearts a bit when they finally hear it.

Every time he barks at a trigger and the trigger goes away, your dog's brain logs a win.

The postman leaves. The van drives off. The man with the dog walks past.

I barked. The threat went. Barking works.

The pathway thickens again.

He has just trained himself, in real time, in your hallway, that the correct response to a doorbell is the response he just gave.

He isn't being naughty. He isn't being disobedient.

He is doing the most natural thing in the world, which is repeating the behaviour his brain keeps telling him is succeeding.

That's the rehearsal loop. And it explains, completely, why every product you tried did nothing.

The part most owners never get told.

Your dog has a ceiling.
Below it, he can think. Above it, he can't.

Every dog has a stress threshold. A line in their nervous system.

The moment it's crossed, the thinking part of the brain switches off. The panic part takes over.

Behaviourists call it the panic line.

Below it, your dog can hear you. Take a treat. Learn something. Make a different choice.

Above it, the amygdala has hijacked the wheel. The rational part of his brain, the part that could learn, is no longer driving.

This is why the cheese didn't work.

He wasn't refusing it. He physically couldn't take it.

His brain had flooded with stress hormones.

The systems that handle "should I take this food" had been switched off, in favour of the systems that handle "is this thing going to kill me."

You weren't standing next to a stubborn dog. You were standing next to a dog whose nervous system had gone into red alert.

And here's the chemistry bit, because it matters.

When a dog goes over the panic line, his brain dumps cortisol into his bloodstream within about fifteen minutes.

Cortisol is a stress hormone. Useful in short bursts, ruinous in sustained ones.

It takes between 24 and 72 hours to fully clear his system.

Read that again, because it changes everything.

The bark you witnessed this morning may have been powered by stress chemistry from Tuesday's Amazon van.

Which means every time you've tried to "correct" his behaviour during or after a bark, you've been trying to teach a dog whose brain was chemically incapable of learning.

You've been shouting at the storm.

You haven't been failing.

The science of how to do this has existed since 1947.

It was first worked out in 1947 by a South African psychiatrist called Joseph Wolpe.

He treated terrified cats by feeding them food at progressively closer distances to the thing that scared them.

He published a 90% success rate in peer-reviewed literature.

The method he invented is called systematic desensitisation paired with counter-conditioning.

It is now the gold standard endorsed by the American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior.

It is used by Guide Dogs UK to prepare working dogs for life on a London pavement.

It is used by military working dog handlers to teach dogs to stay calm under live gunfire.

The same protocol that produces a guide dog who can sleep through Oxford Circus rush hour is the protocol that, scaled down to a kitchen and a phone speaker, will produce a dog who hears the doorbell and looks at you instead of exploding.
